# Module 2 — The Evolution of Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity has evolved as technology has grown and attackers have developed new methods to exploit systems and people. Understanding how attacks work helps security professionals detect and prevent them.

---

# Malware

**Malware** is software designed to harm devices, networks, or users.

Examples of damage malware can cause:

* Data theft
* System disruption
* Financial loss
* Unauthorized access

### Example

* **Equifax Data Breach** — attackers used malware to gain access to sensitive customer data.

---

# Social Engineering

**Social engineering** is a manipulation technique that exploits human error to gain:

* Private information
* System access
* Money or valuables

Instead of attacking systems directly, attackers **target human behavior**.

### Example

* **The Love Letter Attack** used social engineering combined with malware to spread rapidly.

---

# Phishing

**Phishing** is the use of digital communications to trick people into:

* Revealing sensitive information
* Installing malicious software

Attackers often impersonate **trusted sources** such as coworkers, companies, or government agencies.

---

# Common Cyber Attacks

## Phishing Attacks

### Business Email Compromise (BEC)

A **BEC attack** occurs when a threat actor sends an email that appears to come from a trusted source to request sensitive information or money.

Goal:

* Financial gain

---

### Spear Phishing

A **spear phishing attack** targets a specific individual or group.

The attacker researches the target to make the message appear legitimate.

#### Whaling

A form of spear phishing that targets **high-level executives or decision makers**.

---

### Vishing

**Voice phishing (vishing)** uses phone calls or voice communication to trick victims into revealing sensitive information.

Example tactics:

* Impersonating banks
* Impersonating IT support
* Fake government calls

---

### Smishing

**Smishing** uses text messages to trick victims into:

* Clicking malicious links
* Revealing sensitive information

---

# Malware Types

## Viruses

A **virus** is malicious code designed to interfere with computer operations and damage data or software.

Viruses usually require **user interaction**, such as downloading or opening a file.

Examples:

* Brain Virus
* Love Letter Attack

---

## Worms

A **worm** is malware that spreads automatically across systems without needing user interaction.

Worms often spread through **network vulnerabilities**.

Example:

* Morris Worm

---

## Ransomware

**Ransomware** encrypts an organization's data and demands payment to restore access.

Consequences include:

* Data loss
* Financial damage
* Operational disruption

---

## Spyware

**Spyware** secretly collects information from a device without user consent.

Examples of stolen data:

* Personal information
* Emails and text messages
* Voice recordings
* Location data

---

# Social Engineering Attacks

## Social Media Phishing

Attackers collect information from social media profiles to craft targeted phishing attacks.

---

## Watering Hole Attack

In a **watering hole attack**, threat actors compromise a website that a specific group frequently visits.

When users visit the site, their systems become infected.

---

## USB Baiting

Attackers leave infected USB devices in places where employees might find them.

If someone plugs the USB into their computer, malware installs automatically.

---

## Physical Social Engineering

Attackers impersonate legitimate individuals such as:

* Employees
* Vendors
* Customers

Their goal is to gain **unauthorized access to physical locations**.

---

# Social Engineering Principles

Threat actors exploit common psychological behaviors.

## Authority

Attackers impersonate authority figures because people tend to obey individuals with power.

Example:

* Someone pretending to be a company executive.

---

## Intimidation

Attackers use fear or pressure to force victims to act quickly.

---

## Consensus (Social Proof)

People often trust actions that appear to be accepted by others.

Attackers exploit this by claiming others have already approved their request.

---

## Scarcity

Attackers create a sense that something is **limited or running out**.

This pressures victims to act quickly.

---

## Familiarity

Attackers pretend to have a friendly relationship with the victim.

---

## Trust

Threat actors build long-term relationships to slowly gain access to information.

---

## Urgency

Attackers push victims to act immediately without verifying the request.

---

# Data Breach Response

Organizations follow a protocol when a breach occurs.

## Step 1 — Contain the Breach

Stop further data loss by:

* Shutting down servers
* Disconnecting systems
* Isolating affected networks

---

## Step 2 — Investigate the Breach

Security teams analyze the attack to determine:

* How the breach occurred
* What systems were affected
* What data was compromised

---

# CISSP Security Domains

The **Certified Information Systems Security Professional (CISSP)** certification defines eight core domains of cybersecurity knowledge.

Security gaps in these domains can negatively impact organizations.

---

## 1. Security and Risk Management

Defines:

* Security goals
* Risk mitigation strategies
* Legal and regulatory compliance
* Business continuity

Example:

Following **HIPAA regulations**.

---

## 2. Asset Security

Protects digital and physical assets and manages:

* Data storage
* Data maintenance
* Data retention
* Secure destruction

Example:

Destroying confidential documents.

---

## 3. Security Architecture and Engineering

Ensures security tools and systems are properly designed and configured.

Example:

Configuring a firewall.

---

## 4. Communications and Network Security

Focuses on securing network infrastructure and communications.

Examples:

* Monitoring user behavior
* Creating network security policies

---

## 5. Identity and Access Management (IAM)

Ensures users only access resources they are authorized to use.

Example:

Managing employee building keycards or login credentials.

---

## 6. Security Assessment and Testing

Involves testing security controls and identifying vulnerabilities.

Example:

Auditing user permissions.

---

## 7. Security Operations

Focuses on monitoring systems and responding to threats.

Example:

Investigating unknown devices connecting to a network.

---

## 8. Software Development Security

Ensures secure coding practices during application development.

Example:

Working with developers to implement secure authentication systems.

---

# Types of Cyber Attacks

## Password Attacks

Attempts to gain access to systems by cracking passwords.

Examples:

* Brute force attacks
* Rainbow table attacks

Related domain:

* Communications and Network Security

---

## Social Engineering Attacks

Manipulation techniques targeting human behavior.

Examples include:

* Phishing
* Smishing
* Vishing
* Spear phishing
* Whaling
* Watering hole attacks
* USB baiting

Related domain:

* Security and Risk Management

---

## Physical Attacks

Attacks targeting physical systems or environments.

Examples:

* Malicious USB cables
* Malicious flash drives
* Card cloning or skimming

Related domain:

* Asset Security

---

## Adversarial Artificial Intelligence Attacks

Attackers manipulate AI or machine learning systems to conduct more efficient attacks.

Related domains:

* Communications and Network Security
* Identity and Access Management

---

## Supply Chain Attacks

Attackers exploit vulnerabilities in third-party vendors involved in producing software or hardware.

Because supply chains involve multiple organizations, these attacks can affect **many systems simultaneously**.

Related domains:

* Security and Risk Management
* Security Architecture and Engineering
* Security Operations

---

## Cryptographic Attacks

These attacks target encrypted communications.

Examples:

* Birthday attacks
* Collision attacks
* Downgrade attacks

Related domain:

* Communications and Network Security

---

# Threat Actor Types

## Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)

Highly skilled attackers who gain unauthorized access and remain undetected for long periods.

Targets often include:

* Governments
* Large corporations
* Critical infrastructure

Motivations:

* Stealing intellectual property
* Damaging infrastructure

---

## Insider Threats

Individuals with authorized access who misuse their privileges.

Motivations include:

* Sabotage
* Espionage
* Corruption
* Data theft

---

## Hacktivists

Hackers motivated by political or social causes.

Common activities:

* Public demonstrations
* Propaganda
* Social change campaigns

---

# Hacker Types

## Ethical Hackers

Authorized security professionals who test systems for vulnerabilities.

Goal:

* Improve organizational security.

---

## Security Researchers

Researchers who discover vulnerabilities but **do not exploit them**.

---

## Unethical Hackers

Malicious attackers who break into systems for financial gain or personal benefit.

---

# Other Threat Actors

## New or Unskilled Attackers

These attackers may:

* Learn hacking techniques
* Seek revenge
* Use pre-existing malware tools

---

## Vigilante Hackers

Hackers who attempt to stop malicious attackers.

---

## Contract Hackers

Individuals hired to perform hacking activities.

Their work may be **legal or illegal depending on the situation**.

---

# CISSP Certification

The **Certified Information Systems Security Professional (CISSP)** is one of the most respected cybersecurity certifications in the world.

It is awarded by the **International Information System Security Certification Consortium (ISC²)** and demonstrates expertise across the eight security domains.
